Entering Mountain Operations

A polished guide for ASU students built from interviews, course research, and my own experience as a ski instructor. My main finding is that skiing skill matters, but communication is what helps people enter, adapt, and become trusted in this community.

Explore the findings

Who this is for

ASU students interested in seasonal work, outdoor careers, leadership, or learning how a communication-heavy professional community actually works.

Core research finding

Communication is not usually taught in a formal lesson. New members learn it by observing experienced people, getting corrected, and adjusting over time.

Why it matters

Understanding communication practices early helps newcomers integrate faster, perform better, and build credibility within ski school and ski patrol environments.

01

What this community actually is

Mountain operations is more than skiing ability. It is a communication-driven environment made up of ski instructors, ski patrollers, supervisors, and operations staff who rely on shared writing practices, verbal coordination, and structured communication to manage safety, instruction, and guest experience.

My research in one line: technical skill can get someone into the community, but communication is what helps them function inside it.

Why I focused on communication

As a ski instructor, I started noticing that what separated new instructors from experienced ones was not just skiing. It was how clearly they explained skills, how useful their notes were, and how efficiently they coordinated with other staff. That same pattern showed up in my interviews with ski patrol and other instructors.

What my interviews showed

  • ✓Newcomers learn by watching experienced members.
  • ✓Writing like lesson notes and patrol reports helps organize work.
  • ✓Expertise becomes visible through calm, precise communication.

02

How to enter the community

Getting into mountain operations usually starts with technical ability, certifications, and hiring. But my research showed that this is only the beginning. New members also need to learn how the community communicates through lesson notes, reports, radio calls, and feedback from experienced staff.

What surprised me most

Before I started this project, I assumed communication would be clearly taught during training. Instead, it seems to be something people absorb by participating. That means new members have to pay attention not just to how people ski, but to how they explain, document, and coordinate.

How to get started

  1. Build strong skiing ability and earn certifications that fit the role you want, such as PSIA Level 1 for instruction.
  2. Apply early to resorts and expect hiring, shadowing, and on-mountain training to be competitive and fast-paced.
  3. Watch how experienced members write notes, give directions, and communicate with guests or coworkers.
  4. Ask for feedback early and often. Communication improves faster when you treat it like part of the job, not a side task.
  5. Focus on clarity, calmness, and adaptability. Those qualities shape whether people see you as reliable.

What newcomers should know

Entering the community is not just about being able to ski well. It is also about learning the tone, pace, and expectations of a workplace that runs on shared communication.

03

How communication actually works

A key finding from my research is that communication is learned through participation. As a ski instructor, I learned how to write lesson notes not from a manual, but by watching experienced instructors, receiving feedback, and adjusting over time. Ski patrollers seem to develop report-writing in a similar way, through correction and repetition.

Different roles, different expectations

Ski patrol communication is usually more structured, objective, and tied to safety and liability. Ski instruction communication is more adaptive and focused on clarity, tone, and explaining skills in ways that fit each student. So while both groups rely on communication, the purpose changes what that communication looks like.

What defines expertise

One of the biggest takeaways from my research is that expertise is not just about skiing ability. Experienced members communicate more clearly, efficiently, and appropriately for the situation. Whether it is writing stronger lesson notes, explaining skills more effectively, or staying calm and precise on the radio, communication becomes a major marker of expertise.

In practice: the best instructors are usually not just the strongest skiers. They are the ones who can explain, adjust, and document well enough that other people can build on their work.

Key communication takeaways

  • ✓Treat lesson notes and reports as meaningful communication, not busywork.
  • ✓Learn the tone and expectations of the role you want to enter.
  • ✓Build technical skill and communication skill at the same time.

05

Rhetorical Reflection

This website remixes my research argument into a format that is easier for ASU students to use. In my research paper, I wrote for an academic audience and focused on analysis, theory, and evidence. For this website, I shifted to a student-facing audience that needs information quickly and clearly. That change affected nearly every rhetorical decision I made. Instead of long paragraphs and dense source integration, I used shorter sections, navigation tabs, images, and videos so the audience could move through the information in a practical way.

Audience and Purpose

My main audience is ASU students interested in outdoor work and seasonal employment. Because they are not experts, the site focuses on clarity and usability. Sections like “How to Enter” and “Communication” translate research into something actionable.

Genre Choice

I chose a website because it allows for visual and interactive learning. The use of images and videos reinforces the real-world context, while navigation lets users access information quickly instead of reading linearly like an essay.

Use of Research

This remix keeps my core argument that communication defines success in mountain operations. Instead of presenting it through dense academic writing, I adapted it into a format that teaches and guides new members of the discourse community.
