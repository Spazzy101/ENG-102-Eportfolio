Critical Reflection

This rhetorical analysis piece helped me think carefully about audience, purpose, genre, and design before creating a public-facing version of my research. Instead of assuming that my project should only be written for my instructor, I identified a real audience: Arizona State University students interested in seasonal work, outdoor recreation, non-traditional career paths, or hands-on work experience. This helped me define a clearer rhetorical purpose for the website version of my project.

This assignment connects to the outcome of exhibiting flexibility when addressing different audiences and rhetorical situations. A traditional academic essay allows for longer explanation and more formal language, but a website needs clearer organization, shorter sections, visuals, and easy navigation. In the analysis, I explained that students using a website expect quick answers to questions like What is this? and How do I get started? Recognizing that difference helped me adjust my tone and structure for a digital audience.

This piece also connects to the outcome of contributing to an academic debate or issue using culturally specific discourses and argumentative tactics. My project takes research on discourse communities and translates it into a useful format for students who may not know that mountain operations has structured communication practices. It also reflects the politics of fair use and citation because a public website has to use sources, visuals, and evidence responsibly. Overall, this assignment helped me understand that rhetoric is not only about what I say, but how I shape information for a real audience.
