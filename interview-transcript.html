Critical Reflection

The interview transcript was important because it gave my project primary research instead of relying only on personal experience or secondary sources. I interviewed two people connected to the mountain operations discourse community: my father, who discussed ski patrol, and my sister, who discussed ski instruction. Their responses gave me specific evidence about radio communication, incident reports, lesson notes, and how newcomers learn the communication expectations of the community.

This piece connects most clearly to the outcome of implementing composing processes such as invention, collaboration, revision, and reflection. The interview process required me to invent questions, gather responses, and then think about how those responses could support my larger argument. It also helped me practice adopting an open stance toward competing perspectives because patrol and ski instruction have different responsibilities. Patrol emphasizes factual reporting, emergency response, and legal documentation, while instruction emphasizes guests, lesson notes, and positive communication.

One of the most useful parts of this assignment was realizing that both interviewees described writing as something learned through practice and feedback. My father explained that senior patrollers correct unclear or opinion-based reports, while my sister explained that instructors learn lesson notes by seeing examples and receiving feedback from supervisors. This helped me understand that writing in a discourse community is not just taught formally; it is learned through participation. In future professional settings, this reminds me that asking good questions and learning from experienced people will help me adapt more quickly.
